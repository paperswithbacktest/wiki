## What is a Certificate of Deposit (CD)?

A Certificate of Deposit, or CD, is a type of savings account you can open at a bank or credit union. When you put money into a CD, you agree to leave it there for a certain amount of time, called the term. This term can be anywhere from a few months to several years. In return, the bank promises to pay you a fixed interest rate, which is usually higher than what you would get from a regular savings account.

Once you put money into a CD, you generally can't take it out until the term is over. If you do need to withdraw your money early, you might have to pay a penalty. This makes CDs a good choice if you have money you won't need to use right away and want to earn a bit more interest than a regular savings account offers. CDs are also safe because they are insured by the government up to a certain amount, so you don't have to worry about losing your money.

## How do promotional CD rates differ from standard rates?

Promotional CD rates are special interest rates that banks offer for a limited time to attract new customers or to encourage people to invest more money. These rates are usually higher than the standard rates that the bank normally offers. For example, a bank might advertise a promotional rate of 3% for a 12-month CD, while their standard rate for the same term might be only 2%. The goal is to make the CD look more appealing and get people to open an account quickly before the promotion ends.

However, there are some things to keep in mind with promotional rates. They often come with certain conditions, like a minimum deposit amount that might be higher than what's required for standard CDs. Also, once the promotional period is over, your CD might automatically renew at the lower standard rate unless you take action. So, it's important to read the fine print and understand the terms before you decide to go for a promotional rate.
