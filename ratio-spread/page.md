## What is a ratio spread in options trading?

A ratio spread in options trading is a strategy where you buy and sell different numbers of options at different strike prices. For example, you might buy one call option and sell two call options at a higher strike price. This creates an imbalance, or ratio, between the number of options bought and sold. The goal is often to profit from the difference in how quickly the value of the options changes as the price of the underlying asset moves.

This strategy can be used with both call and put options. It's often used when a trader has a specific view on the direction and volatility of the underlying asset. For example, if a trader thinks a stock will go up a little bit but not too much, they might use a call ratio spread. The main risk with a ratio spread is that if the underlying asset moves too far in the wrong direction, the trader could lose more money than they initially put in. So, it's important to manage the risk carefully.

## How does a ratio spread differ from a regular spread?

A ratio spread and a regular spread are both options trading strategies, but they work a bit differently. A regular spread, like a vertical spread, involves buying and selling the same number of options, but at different strike prices. For example, you might buy one call option and sell one call option at a higher strike price. The goal is usually to make money from the price difference between the two options as the underlying asset moves.

A ratio spread, on the other hand, involves buying and selling a different number of options. For instance, you might buy one call option and sell two call options at a higher strike price. This creates a ratio, like 1:2, which is where the name comes from. The idea is to profit from the different rates at which the options' values change. But, because you're selling more options than you're buying, there's more risk if the underlying asset moves a lot in the wrong direction.
