## What are Regulatory Accounting Principles (RAP)?

Regulatory Accounting Principles (RAP) are a set of rules and guidelines that companies in certain industries, like insurance and utilities, have to follow. These rules are set by government agencies to make sure these companies keep good financial records and act fairly. RAP helps these companies show their financial health in a clear way, so regulators can check if they are doing well and following the law.

RAP can be different from Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP), which are used by most other businesses. While GAAP focuses on giving a true picture of a company's financial situation for investors, RAP focuses more on making sure the company follows the rules set by regulators. This means that the same company might show different financial results under RAP and GAAP, depending on what the regulators need to see.

## How do Regulatory Accounting Principles differ from Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP)?

Regulatory Accounting Principles (RAP) and Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP) are two different sets of rules that companies use to keep their financial records. RAP is used by companies in certain industries, like insurance and utilities, and is set by government agencies. The main goal of RAP is to make sure these companies follow the rules and show their financial health in a way that regulators can easily check. On the other hand, GAAP is used by most other businesses and is set by the Financial Accounting Standards Board (FASB). GAAP focuses on giving investors and others a true picture of a company's financial situation.

Because RAP and GAAP have different goals, the same company might show different financial results under each set of rules. For example, under RAP, a utility company might have to report certain costs differently to show regulators how it is managing its operations and meeting regulatory requirements. In contrast, under GAAP, the same company would report those costs in a way that gives investors a clear view of its profitability and financial health. This difference can lead to different financial statements, which is why companies in regulated industries often have to keep two sets of [books](/wiki/algo-trading-books).
