## What are weekly options?

Weekly options are a type of financial contract that expire every week, instead of the usual monthly cycle. They are used by traders to bet on the short-term movements of stocks, indexes, or other financial products. Because they expire quickly, weekly options can be riskier but also offer the chance for quick profits if the market moves in the right direction.

These options are popular among traders who want to take advantage of short-term market events or news. For example, if a company is about to release earnings, a trader might use weekly options to make a quick trade based on the expected outcome. Weekly options can be more affordable than monthly options because they are cheaper to buy, but they also have less time for the market to move in your favor.

## How do weekly options differ from monthly options?

Weekly options and monthly options are both types of financial contracts, but they have different expiration times. Weekly options expire every week, usually on Fridays. This means they last for a shorter time than monthly options, which expire at the end of each month. Because weekly options expire faster, they can be riskier but also give traders a chance to make quick profits if the market moves the right way.

Monthly options, on the other hand, give traders more time for the market to move in their favor. They are often used for longer-term bets on the market. Since they last longer, monthly options are usually more expensive than weekly options. Traders might choose monthly options if they want to hold a position for a longer period and are willing to pay more for that extra time.

## What are the tax implications of trading weekly options?

When you trade weekly options, you need to think about taxes. In the United States, the money you make from trading options is usually taxed as capital gains. If you hold the option for less than a year before selling it, any profit you make is considered a short-term capital gain. Short-term capital gains are taxed at the same rate as your regular income, which can be as high as 37% depending on how much money you make. If you hold the option for more than a year, it's a long-term capital gain, which is taxed at a lower rate, usually between 0% and 20%.

But weekly options are usually held for less than a year, so they are almost always taxed as short-term capital gains. Also, if you're selling options to make income, the money you get from selling the options is taxed as ordinary income.
