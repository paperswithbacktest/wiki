## What is the zero bound in monetary policy?

The zero bound in monetary policy refers to a situation where a central bank can't lower interest rates any further because they are already at or near zero percent. When interest rates are at this level, traditional monetary policy tools, like cutting rates to stimulate the economy, become ineffective. This happens because people and businesses can't earn any interest on their savings, and borrowing costs can't go any lower.

When a country's economy faces the zero bound, central banks might use other methods to help the economy. These methods can include buying government bonds or other assets to increase the money supply, a policy known as quantitative easing. They might also use forward guidance, where they communicate future policy actions to influence economic expectations. These alternative strategies aim to encourage spending and investment even when interest rates can't be lowered further.

## Why is the zero bound significant for central banks?

The zero bound is important for central banks because it limits what they can do to help the economy. When interest rates are at zero, central banks can't use their usual tool of lowering rates to make borrowing cheaper and encourage spending. This is a problem because when the economy is struggling, people and businesses might need that extra push to start spending and investing again.

Because of the zero bound, central banks have to find other ways to help the economy. They might start buying bonds or other assets to put more money into the economy, which is called quantitative easing. They could also tell everyone what they plan to do in the future, which is called forward guidance. These methods can help, but they're not as straightforward as just changing interest rates, so the zero bound makes things more complicated for central banks.

## What are negative interest rates and how do they bypass the zero bound?
