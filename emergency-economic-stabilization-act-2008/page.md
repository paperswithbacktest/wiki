## What is the Emergency Economic Stabilization Act of 2008?

The Emergency Economic Stabilization Act of 2008 was a law passed by the United States Congress to help fix the problems in the economy during the financial crisis. It was signed into law by President George W. Bush on October 3, 2008. The main part of this law created the Troubled Asset Relief Program (TARP), which allowed the government to spend up to $700 billion to buy bad loans and other financial assets from banks and other financial institutions. This was done to help these institutions stay stable and keep lending money to people and businesses.

The Act also included other measures to help the economy. It raised the limit on how much money the government could insure in bank accounts, from $100,000 to $250,000. This was to make people feel safer about keeping their money in banks. Additionally, the Act provided tax breaks for businesses and individuals to encourage spending and investment. Overall, the Emergency Economic Stabilization Act was a big effort by the government to calm the financial markets and help the economy recover from the crisis.
