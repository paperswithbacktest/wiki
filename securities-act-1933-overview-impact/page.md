## What is the Securities Act of 1933?

The Securities Act of 1933 is a law in the United States that was created to protect investors. It was passed after the stock market crash of 1929, which led to the Great Depression. The main goal of this law is to make sure that companies tell the truth about their businesses when they want to sell stocks or bonds to the public. This law requires companies to give important information to the public through a document called a prospectus. This helps investors make better decisions by knowing more about the company they are investing in.

The Securities Act of 1933 also includes rules about how securities can be sold. It says that all securities must be registered with the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) before they can be sold to the public. This registration process involves the company providing detailed information about its finances, the securities being offered, and the risks involved. There are some exceptions to this rule, known as exemptions, where certain types of securities do not need to be registered. Overall, this law helps to create a more honest and transparent market for investors.

## What is the difference between the Securities Act of 1933 and the Securities Exchange Act of 1934?
