## What is a coupon bond?

A coupon bond is a type of bond that pays interest to the bondholder at regular intervals, usually every six months or annually, until the bond matures. The interest payment is called a coupon, which is why it's called a coupon bond. When you buy a coupon bond, you are essentially lending money to the issuer, which could be a government or a company, and in return, they promise to pay you back the amount you lent them, plus interest, over time.

When a coupon bond is issued, it has a face value, which is the amount the issuer agrees to repay at the end of the bond's term, known as the maturity date. The coupon rate is a percentage of the face value that determines how much interest you will receive each period. For example, if a bond has a face value of $1,000 and a coupon rate of 5%, you would receive $50 every year until the bond matures. At maturity, you get the face value back, so in this case, you would receive $1,000. Coupon bonds are popular because they provide a steady stream of income, making them attractive to investors looking for regular payments.

## What is a zero-coupon bond?

A zero-coupon bond is a type of bond that doesn't pay interest during its term. Instead, it is sold at a discount from its face value, and when it matures, the bondholder receives the full face value. For example, you might buy a zero-coupon bond for $800, but when it matures, you get $1,000. The difference between what you paid and what you get at the end is the interest you earn.

Because zero-coupon bonds don't make regular interest payments, they are often used by investors who want to save for a specific future date, like when they plan to pay for college or retire. These bonds are also simpler to manage since there are no payments to keep track of until the bond matures. However, because you don't receive any money until the end, you need to be sure you can wait until then to get your money back.

## How do coupon bonds and zero-coupon bonds differ in terms of interest payments?

Coupon bonds and zero-coupon bonds work differently when it comes to interest payments. Coupon bonds pay interest regularly, usually every six months or once a year. This means if you own a coupon bond, you'll get payments throughout the time you hold the bond, until it reaches its maturity date. The amount you get each time is based on the bond's face value and its coupon rate. For example, a bond with a face value of $1,000 and a 5% coupon rate will pay you $50 every year.

On the other hand, zero-coupon bonds do not make any interest payments during their term. Instead, you buy them at a lower price than their face value, and when the bond matures, you get the full face value. The difference between what you paid and what you get at the end is your interest. For instance, if you buy a zero-coupon bond for $800 and its face value is $1,000, you'll receive $1,000 when it matures, and the $200 difference is your interest. This makes zero-coupon bonds good for saving towards a future goal, since you won't get any money until the bond matures.

## What are the typical maturities for coupon bonds versus zero-coupon bonds?

Coupon bonds usually have maturities that range from a few years to 30 years. This means you might buy a coupon bond that pays you interest every year for 5 years, 10 years, or even up to 30 years. The longer the maturity, the more interest payments you'll get over time. Governments and companies often issue these longer-term bonds because they need to borrow money for big projects or long-term plans.

Zero-coupon bonds can have a wide range of maturities too, but they are often issued with shorter terms than coupon bonds. You might find zero-coupon bonds that mature in a few months, a year, or up to 10 or 20 years. Because they don't pay interest along the way, people often use them to save for a specific date in the future, like paying for college or a big purchase. This makes them popular for shorter-term savings goals compared to coupon bonds.

## How does the pricing of coupon bonds compare to zero-coupon bonds?

Coupon bonds are priced based on their face value and the interest rate they offer, called the coupon rate. When you buy a coupon bond, you pay close to its face value, and then you get interest payments every year or every six months. The price can go up or down depending on what's happening in the market, like changes in interest rates. If interest rates go up, the price of the bond might go down because new bonds will offer higher interest. If interest rates go down, the price of the bond might go up because your bond's interest payments are now more attractive compared to new bonds.

Zero-coupon bonds are priced differently because they don't pay interest until they mature. You buy them at a discount from their face value, and when the bond matures, you get the full face value. The difference between what you paid and what you get at the end is your interest. For example, if you buy a zero-coupon bond for $800 and its face value is $1,000, you'll get $1,000 when it matures, and the $200 difference is your interest. The price of a zero-coupon bond also changes with market interest rates, but since you're not getting any payments until the end, it can be more sensitive to these changes.

## What are the tax implications of investing in coupon bonds versus zero-coupon bonds?

When you invest in coupon bonds, you have to pay taxes on the interest you get every year or every six months. The interest you receive from these bonds is considered taxable income. So, even if you don't get the money until the bond matures, you still have to report the interest on your taxes each year and pay taxes on it. This can be a bit tricky because you're paying taxes on money you haven't actually spent yet.

Zero-coupon bonds are different because you don't get any interest payments until the bond matures. But, the IRS still wants you to pay taxes on the interest you're earning each year, even though you don't see it until the end. This is called "imputed interest," and you have to report it on your taxes every year as if you were getting payments. This can be confusing because you're paying taxes on money you won't have until the bond matures.

## How do coupon bonds and zero-coupon bonds react differently to interest rate changes?

Coupon bonds change in price when interest rates go up or down. If interest rates go up, new bonds will have higher interest rates, so people might not want to buy your old bond with a lower rate. This makes the price of your bond go down. If interest rates go down, your bond's interest rate looks better compared to new bonds, so people might want to buy it, and the price goes up. Because coupon bonds pay interest regularly, they are a bit less sensitive to interest rate changes compared to zero-coupon bonds.

Zero-coupon bonds are more sensitive to changes in interest rates. Since they don't pay any interest until they mature, their price depends a lot on what people think interest rates will do in the future. If interest rates go up, the price of a zero-coupon bond will drop a lot because the interest you get at the end will be worth less compared to what you could get from new bonds. If interest rates go down, the price of a zero-coupon bond can go up a lot because the interest you get at the end will be worth more. This makes zero-coupon bonds riskier when interest rates are changing.

## What are the reinvestment risks associated with coupon bonds and zero-coupon bonds?

Coupon bonds have a reinvestment risk because they pay you interest regularly. This means you get money every year or every six months, and you need to find a way to use that money. If interest rates go down after you buy your bond, you might have to put your interest payments into new investments that don't pay as much. This can mean you end up with less money overall than you hoped for. So, with coupon bonds, you have to think about what you'll do with the interest payments and how changes in interest rates might affect your plans.

Zero-coupon bonds don't have this reinvestment risk because they don't pay any interest until they mature. You buy the bond at a lower price and then get the full amount at the end, so you don't have to worry about what to do with interest payments along the way. This can be good if you're saving for something specific in the future and don't want to deal with reinvesting money. But, it also means you don't get to use any of the money until the bond is done, which might not be what everyone wants.

## How do the yield calculations differ between coupon bonds and zero-coupon bonds?

The yield of a coupon bond is calculated based on the interest payments you get every year or every six months. It's like figuring out how much money you're making from the bond each year, compared to what you paid for it. If you buy a bond for $1,000 and it pays you $50 every year, your yield is 5%. But, if the price of the bond goes up or down, the yield changes too. If you buy the same bond for $900, your yield goes up because you're getting the same $50 but you paid less for the bond.

The yield of a zero-coupon bond is different because it doesn't pay any interest until it matures. You buy the bond at a lower price and then get the full amount at the end. The yield is calculated by figuring out what interest rate would make the price you paid grow to the face value by the time the bond matures. For example, if you buy a zero-coupon bond for $800 and it's worth $1,000 when it matures in 5 years, you can work out the yield by seeing what interest rate would turn $800 into $1,000 over 5 years. This makes the yield calculation a bit more complicated than for coupon bonds.

## What are Zero-Coupon Bonds and How Do They Work?

Zero-coupon bonds, also known as discount bonds, are a unique type of fixed-income security that diverges from traditional, interest-bearing bonds by not offering periodic interest payments to investors. Instead, these bonds are initially sold at a price significantly lower than their face value. At maturity, the bondholder receives a single payment equal to the bond's face value. This structure inherently ensures that the potential for return is embedded in the difference between the purchase price and the maturity value.

For investors, zero-coupon bonds present particular appeal due to their predictable nature and potential for significant returns, especially when held to maturity. They are often utilized for long-term financial objectives, such as saving for education or retirement, given their capacity to provide a lump sum in the future. This makes them attractive for individuals looking to grow their wealth over time without the necessity of periodic income.

### Mechanics of Zero-Coupon Bonds

To understand how zero-coupon bonds work, consider the bond's pricing formula:

$$
P = \frac{F}{(1 + r)^n}
$$

where:
- $P$ is the present price of the bond,
- $F$ is the face value of the bond,
- $r$ is the annual yield or interest rate,
- $n$ is the number of years to maturity.

The bond is purchased at $P$, and the holder will receive $F$ at maturity. The return on investment is determined by the difference between $F$ and $P$, compounded over the bond's duration.

### Benefits of Zero-Coupon Bonds

Zero-coupon bonds offer distinct advantages. Firstly, they provide certainty regarding future cash flows, which is especially beneficial for investors aiming at meeting specific financial goals. Additionally, because no periodic interest is paid, investors do not face the risk of having to reinvest coupon payments at potentially lower interest rates, which can affect overall returns.

### Potential Drawbacks

Despite their advantages, zero-coupon bonds are not without risks. Primarily, they are susceptible to [interest rate](/wiki/interest-rate-trading-strategies) fluctuations. As interest rates rise, the price of existing zero-coupon bonds typically falls, given their fixed yield. This interest rate risk is especially pertinent when an investor needs to sell the bond before maturity, potentially realizing a loss if rates have increased.

Another consideration is the tax implication in certain jurisdictions. Although no cash interest is paid until maturity, bondholders may incur tax liabilities annually on the implied interest, known as "phantom income." This can present cash flow challenges unless managed carefully.

### Interest Rate Risk and Reinvestment Risk

Zero-coupon bonds mitigate reinvestment risk, as there are no periodic cash flows that require reinvestment at current interest rates. However, the absence of periodic payments means that all value is tied to the bond's maturity, increasing exposure to interest rate risk over the bond's life. Investors must therefore consider their willingness and ability to hold the bond until maturity to avoid potential capital losses.

In summary, zero-coupon bonds offer a structured path to achieving long-term investment goals, providing clear advantages in terms of capital growth. However, understanding and managing the associated risks, primarily those related to interest rates and taxation, is essential for maximizing their benefits.
