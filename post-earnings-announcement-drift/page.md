## What is a post-earnings-announcement drift?

A post-earnings-announcement drift, often called PEAD, is when a company's stock price keeps moving in the same direction after it announces its earnings. If the earnings are better than expected, the stock price might keep going up for a while. If the earnings are worse than expected, the stock price might keep going down.

This happens because it takes time for all investors to get the new information and react to it. Some investors might not see the earnings report right away, or they might need time to understand what it means. So, the stock price slowly adjusts as more and more people learn about the earnings and decide to buy or sell the stock.

## How does post-earnings-announcement drift affect stock prices?

On the other hand, if a company's earnings are lower than expected, the stock price might keep falling. This drift can last for a while because it takes time for all investors to react. Some might be slow to see the news, while others might need to do more research before deciding to buy or sell the stock. So, the stock price slowly adjusts as more people learn about the earnings and make their moves.

This showed that it takes time for everyone to get the news and react to it.

More recent studies have also found the same thing. For instance, a study by Bernard and Thomas in 1989 looked at how stocks moved after earnings announcements over many years. This means that investors who knew about the drift could make money by buying stocks with good earnings and selling stocks with bad earnings. All these studies show that post-earnings-announcement drift is a real thing that happens in the stock market.

## How have academic theories evolved to explain post-earnings-announcement drift?

Academic theories about post-earnings-announcement drift have changed over time as researchers have learned more about how the stock market works. At first, people thought the drift happened because some investors didn't get the earnings news right away. They called this the "information delay" theory. It said that the stock price kept moving because it took time for everyone to find out about the earnings and decide what to do. But as more studies were done, researchers found that this wasn't the whole story.

Later theories started to focus on how investors understand and react to earnings news. One popular idea is the "behavioral finance" theory. This theory says that investors can be slow to change their minds, even when they see new information. They might need time to understand what the earnings mean for the company's future. This slow reaction can make the stock price keep moving in the same direction for a while. These newer theories help explain why the drift can last for weeks or even months after the earnings are announced.
