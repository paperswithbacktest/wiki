## What is the Theoretical Ex-Rights Price (TERP)?

The Theoretical Ex-Rights Price (TERP) is a way to figure out what a stock's price might be after a company issues new shares through a rights offering. When a company does a rights offering, it gives its current shareholders the right to buy more shares at a lower price before they're offered to the public. The TERP helps investors guess the new stock price by taking into account the number of new shares and their price, along with the current stock price.

To calculate the TERP, you add the total value of the company before the rights offering to the money raised from the new shares, and then divide that by the total number of shares after the new shares are issued. This gives you an idea of what the stock price might be once the rights offering is over and the new shares start trading. It's a useful tool for investors to understand how the rights offering might affect the stock's value.

## How is the Theoretical Ex-Rights Price calculated?

The Theoretical Ex-Rights Price (TERP) is a way to estimate the price of a stock after a company does a rights offering. A rights offering is when a company lets its current shareholders buy more shares at a special lower price before anyone else can. To figure out the TERP, you need to know the current stock price, how many new shares are being offered, and the price of those new shares. The idea is to mix the old value of the company with the new money coming in from the new shares, and then spread that total value over all the shares, both old and new.

To calculate the TERP, you start by adding up the total value of the company before the rights offering. This is just the current stock price times the number of shares already out there. Then, you add the money that will come from selling the new shares. This is the number of new shares times the price they're being offered at. After you have this total value, you divide it by the total number of shares that will be around after the rights offering, which is the old shares plus the new shares. The result is the TERP, which gives you an idea of what the stock price might be once the new shares start trading.

## Why is TERP important for investors?

TERP is important for investors because it helps them understand how a rights offering might change the value of their stock. When a company does a rights offering, it's like adding more pieces to a pie. If the pie stays the same size but you add more pieces, each piece gets smaller. TERP gives investors a way to guess how much smaller their piece of the pie might get after the new shares are added. This helps them decide if they want to buy the new shares, sell their current shares, or do nothing.

Knowing the TERP also helps investors figure out if the rights offering is a good deal. If the TERP is much lower than the current stock price, it might mean the stock will be worth less after the new shares are issued. This could make investors think twice about buying more shares or holding onto what they have. On the other hand, if the TERP is close to the current price, it might not change the value of their investment much, which could be a good sign. So, TERP is a useful tool for making smart investment choices during a rights offering.

## What are the limitations of using TERP in investment decisions?

## What is the Calculation of TERP?

The calculation of the Theoretical Ex-Rights Price (TERP) is essential in understanding the potential shift in stock value following a rights issue. Rights issues involve offering existing shareholders the opportunity to purchase additional shares at a reduced price. This typically results in dilution of the current stock value, and TERP serves as a valuable estimate of the new share price post-issue.

The formula to determine TERP is:

$$
\text{TERP} = \frac{(\text{Number of Existing Shares} \times \text{Market Price}) + (\text{Number of New Shares} \times \text{Subscription Price})}{\text{Total Number of Shares After Issue}}
$$

Each element of this formula is critical:

1. **Number of Existing Shares**: This represents the shares that are currently outstanding before the rights issue.

2. **Market Price**: The current trading price of the stock, which reflects its value before the new shares are offered.

3. **Number of New Shares**: The additional shares that are being offered to existing shareholders as part of the rights issue.

4. **Subscription Price**: The price at which the new shares are offered to shareholders, generally set below the market price to encourage participation.

5. **Total Number of Shares After Issue**: This is the sum of existing and new shares and is used to average out the total value across all shares.

By plugging these values into the formula, investors can anticipate the adjusted stock price. This calculated TERP assists investors in making informed decisions by highlighting how the share price might adjust following the full exercise of the rights issue. This process accounts for the weighted average of existing shares at the market price and new shares at the discounted subscription price, thus enabling a comprehensive view of the potential stock value.
