## What are Treasury securities?

Treasury securities are a type of investment issued by the U.S. government to raise money to fund its operations and pay off debts. They are considered very safe because they are backed by the full faith and credit of the U.S. government. There are three main types of Treasury securities: Treasury bills, Treasury notes, and Treasury bonds. Each type has a different length of time until it matures, which can range from a few weeks to 30 years.

When you buy a Treasury security, you are essentially lending money to the government. In return, the government promises to pay you back the full amount you invested, known as the principal, when the security matures. Additionally, you will receive interest payments at regular intervals, usually every six months. The interest rate is set when you buy the security and remains the same until it matures. This makes Treasury securities a popular choice for investors looking for a low-risk way to save money and earn a steady income.

## What types of Treasury securities exist?

Treasury securities come in three main types: Treasury bills, Treasury notes, and Treasury bonds. Treasury bills, or T-bills, are short-term securities that mature in a few days to one year. They are sold at a discount, which means you buy them for less than their face value, and when they mature, you get the full face value. The difference between what you paid and what you get back is your interest.

Treasury notes, or T-notes, are medium-term securities that mature in 2 to 10 years. When you buy a T-note, you pay the full face value, and you receive interest payments every six months until the note matures. At maturity, you get your original investment back. T-notes are popular because they offer a steady income over a longer period than T-bills.

Treasury bonds, or T-bonds, are long-term securities that mature in 20 to 30 years. Like T-notes, you buy them at face value and receive interest payments every six months. These bonds are good for investors who want to hold onto their investment for a long time and receive regular interest payments. All these securities are backed by the U.S. government, making them very safe investments.

## Who issues Treasury securities?

Treasury securities are issued by the U.S. Department of the Treasury. This department is part of the U.S. government and is in charge of managing the country's money.

When the government needs to borrow money, the Treasury Department creates and sells these securities to people and organizations. This helps the government pay for things like building roads, running schools, and other important projects.

## What are the risks associated with investing in Treasury securities?

Investing in Treasury securities is very safe because they are backed by the U.S. government. This means that the chance of the government not paying you back is very low. However, there are still some risks to think about. One risk is interest rate risk. This means that if interest rates go up after you buy a Treasury security, the value of your security might go down. If you need to sell it before it matures, you might get less money back than you expected.

Another risk is inflation risk. Inflation is when the prices of things go up over time. If inflation goes up a lot, the interest you earn on your Treasury securities might not be enough to keep up with the rising prices. This means that the money you get back might not be worth as much as when you first invested it. Even though these risks are there, Treasury securities are still seen as very safe investments because the U.S. government promises to pay you back.

## What is the difference between nominal and inflation-protected Treasury securities?

Nominal Treasury securities are the regular ones that you can buy from the government. They pay you a fixed amount of interest every six months until they mature. The interest rate stays the same no matter what happens in the economy. So, if you buy a nominal Treasury security, you know exactly how much interest you will get. The problem is, if inflation goes up, the money you get back might not be worth as much as when you first invested it.

Inflation-protected Treasury securities, also called TIPS (Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities), are different. They are designed to protect you from inflation. The principal amount of TIPS goes up or down with inflation. So, if inflation goes up, the principal of your TIPS goes up too. This means the interest payments you get every six months will also be higher because they are based on the new, higher principal. When TIPS mature, you get the adjusted principal back, which helps make sure your money keeps its value even if prices go up.

## How do changes in interest rates impact Treasury securities?

When interest rates change, it can affect Treasury securities in a big way. If interest rates go up after you buy a Treasury security, the value of your security might go down. This is because new Treasury securities will be sold with higher interest rates, making your older security less attractive to other investors. If you need to sell your security before it matures, you might have to sell it for less than you paid for it. This is called interest rate risk.

On the other hand, if interest rates go down after you buy a Treasury security, the value of your security might go up. This is because your security now pays a higher interest rate than new securities being sold. If you want to sell it before it matures, you might be able to sell it for more than you paid for it. But if you hold onto it until it matures, you will still get the same amount of interest you were promised when you bought it. So, changes in interest rates can make the value of Treasury securities go up or down, depending on whether rates go up or down after you buy them.

This means you have to pay federal income tax on the interest you get every year. But there's good news: the interest from Treasury securities is not taxed by states or local governments. So, you don't have to worry about state or local taxes on that interest.

If you buy and sell Treasury securities before they mature, you might also have to pay capital gains tax. Capital gains tax is what you pay on the profit you make from selling something for more than you paid for it. But if you hold onto your Treasury securities until they mature, you won't have to worry about capital gains tax because you'll get back the same amount you invested, plus the interest you've already paid taxes on. So, it's important to think about both the interest you earn and any potential capital gains when figuring out the tax implications of investing in Treasury securities.

## What is the understanding of Government Bonds?

Government bonds are a fundamental component of global finance, representing debt securities issued by a government to support its spending commitments. These instruments are pivotal in guiding economic stability and fiscal management for nations. Due to the backing by the issuing government's creditworthiness, government bonds are widely regarded as low-risk investments, providing a relatively secure option compared to corporate bonds or equities.

The primary types of government bonds include Treasury Bills (T-Bills), Treasury Notes (T-Notes), and Treasury Bonds (T-Bonds), each offering distinctive features concerning maturity and yield. T-Bills are short-term securities, typically maturing in less than one year. They do not pay interest but are sold at a discount, meaning investors receive the face value at maturity. The difference between the purchase price and the face value reflects the implied interest earned.

T-Notes have maturities ranging from two to ten years and provide investors with periodic interest payments, known as coupons, throughout their term. These payments make them attractive to those seeking a balance between risk and returns over a medium-term horizon. T-Bonds, the long-term option in government securities, have maturities extending up to 30 years. They offer fixed interest payments every six months, providing investors with a stable income stream over decades.

Mathematically, the valuation of government bonds can be approached using present value formulas. The present value $PV$ of a bond that pays periodic coupons can be calculated as follows:

$$

PV = \sum_{t=1}^{n} \frac{C}{(1 + r)^t} + \frac{F}{(1 + r)^n} 
$$

Here, $C$ represents the annual coupon payment, $r$ is the discount rate, $t$ is the time period, $n$ is the total number of periods, and $F$ denotes the face value of the bond.

These government-issued bonds serve dual purposes. Firstly, they provide a secure investment avenue for individuals and institutions seeking to mitigate risks associated with market [volatility](/wiki/volatility-trading-strategies). Secondly, they are critical for government fiscal policy, helping manage national debt, control inflation, and stabilize the economy. Through prudent issuance and management of these bonds, governments can effectively finance infrastructure projects, public services, and other significant expenditures, sustaining economic growth and stability over time.
