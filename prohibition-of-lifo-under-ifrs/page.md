## What is LIFO?

LIFO stands for Last In, First Out. It's a way of organizing and managing things where the last item you add is the first one you take out. Imagine a stack of books. If you keep adding new books on top, the last book you put on top is the first one you'll take off when you need a book. This method is often used in accounting and inventory management.

In accounting, LIFO is used to value inventory. When a company sells products, it assumes that the most recently purchased items are sold first. This can affect the cost of goods sold and the company's profits, especially if prices are changing. For example, if prices are going up, using LIFO can result in higher costs of goods sold and lower profits, which can be beneficial for tax purposes.

## What is IFRS?

IFRS stands for International Financial Reporting Standards. These are rules that companies around the world use to make their financial statements. The goal is to make it easier for people to understand and compare financial information from different countries. IFRS is created by an organization called the International Accounting Standards Board (IASB).

Using IFRS helps make financial reports more clear and consistent. This is important for investors, because it helps them make better decisions about where to put their money. It also helps companies that do business in many countries, because they can use the same accounting rules everywhere. IFRS covers many topics, like how to report income, expenses, assets, and liabilities.

## Why was LIFO prohibited under IFRS?

LIFO, which stands for Last In, First Out, was banned under IFRS because it can make a company's financial reports less accurate. When prices are going up, LIFO can make it look like a company is spending more on goods than it really is. This happens because LIFO says the newest, most expensive items are sold first. This can make profits look smaller than they really are, which can be confusing for people trying to understand the company's financial health.

IFRS wants financial reports to be clear and easy to compare. By not allowing LIFO, IFRS makes sure that all companies report their inventory costs in a similar way. This helps investors and others see a true picture of a company's performance. Other methods like FIFO (First In, First Out) or weighted average cost are used instead, which are seen as more accurate and fair.

## What are the alternatives to LIFO under IFRS?

Under IFRS, the main alternative to LIFO is FIFO, which stands for First In, First Out. FIFO means that the oldest items in inventory are sold first. Imagine a shelf of cans in a store. If you keep adding new cans to the back, the cans at the front (the oldest ones) are sold first. This method can give a clearer picture of a company's costs and profits, especially when prices are going up.

Another method allowed under IFRS is the weighted average cost method. This method calculates the average cost of all the items in inventory and uses that average to figure out the cost of goods sold. It's like mixing all your different priced items together and then using the average price to value what you sell. This can be easier to use than FIFO because it doesn't require keeping track of which items came in first.

Both FIFO and the weighted average cost method are seen as more fair and accurate than LIFO. They help make sure that financial reports show a true picture of a company's inventory costs and profits, which is important for investors and others who need to understand the company's financial health.
