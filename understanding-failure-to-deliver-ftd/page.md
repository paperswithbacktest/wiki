## What is Failure to Deliver (FTD) in simple terms?

Failure to Deliver (FTD) happens when someone who is supposed to give stocks or securities to another person doesn't do it on time. This can happen in the stock market when a seller promises to give shares to a buyer but fails to do so by the agreed date. It's like promising to lend a friend a book but not giving it to them when you said you would.

FTDs can cause problems in the stock market because they can make it hard to know if trades are real or not. When there are a lot of FTDs, it can shake people's trust in the market. To fix this, rules and systems are in place to track and reduce FTDs, making sure that trades happen smoothly and fairly.

## How does FTD occur in the stock market?

FTDs in the stock market can happen when someone sells a stock they don't actually own yet. This is called short selling. The seller borrows the stock from someone else to sell it, promising to give it back later. If they can't find the stock to return it by the deadline, an FTD happens. It's like borrowing a toy from a friend to trade with someone else, but then not being able to get the toy back to return it on time.

Another way FTDs can occur is if there are mistakes or delays in the process of moving stocks from one place to another. Sometimes, the systems that handle these transfers can have problems, or there might be issues with the paperwork needed to complete the trade. When these things go wrong, the stock doesn't get delivered on time, leading to an FTD. It's similar to mailing a package that gets lost or delayed in the mail, so it doesn't arrive when it's supposed to.

## What measures are in place to prevent or reduce FTD?

## How is FTD reported and tracked by regulatory bodies?

FTDs are reported and tracked by regulatory bodies like the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC). They use a system called the Consolidated Audit Trail (CAT) to keep an eye on all trades in the market. This system collects data about every trade, including when stocks are supposed to be delivered and if they actually are. If a stock isn't delivered on time, it gets flagged as an FTD. This helps the SEC see where problems are happening and make sure rules are followed.

The SEC also gets regular reports from places like the Financial Industry Regulatory Authority (FINRA). FINRA makes sure that brokers and dealers follow the rules about delivering stocks. They collect data on FTDs and send it to the SEC. This helps the SEC keep track of how often FTDs happen and if they're getting better or worse. By watching this data, the SEC can step in if they need to, to make the market safer and fairer for everyone.

## What are the differences between FTD in stocks versus other securities like bonds or options?

FTD can happen with stocks, bonds, and options, but the way it works and the rules around it can be a bit different. For stocks, FTD happens when someone sells a stock they don't own yet and can't deliver it on time. This is often linked to short selling, where a trader borrows a stock to sell it, hoping to buy it back cheaper later. If they can't get the stock back in time, an FTD happens. The rules, like Regulation SHO, are strict about making sure stocks are delivered on time to keep the market fair.

For bonds, FTD can happen if someone sells a bond and can't deliver it by the agreed date. Bonds are different from stocks because they are loans to a company or government, and they usually have a set time to be delivered. If there's a delay in getting the bond to the buyer, it can cause an FTD. The rules for bonds might not be as strict as for stocks, but there are still systems in place to track and fix these problems.

Options are another type of security where FTD can happen. An option gives someone the right to buy or sell a stock at a certain price. If someone sells an option and can't deliver the stock when the option is used, it can lead to an FTD. Options have their own set of rules and systems to make sure they are handled correctly. While the basic idea of FTD is the same across stocks, bonds, and options, the details and rules can be different for each one.
