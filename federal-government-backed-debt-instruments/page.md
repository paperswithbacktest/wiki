## What is a federal government-backed debt instrument?

A federal government-backed debt instrument is a type of loan that the government promises to pay back. It's like when you borrow money from someone and promise to give it back later, but in this case, it's the government borrowing money from people or organizations. These instruments are considered very safe because the government is behind them, which means they are less likely to fail to pay back the money.

These debt instruments can come in different forms, like bonds or treasury bills. People or organizations buy these from the government, and in return, the government pays them back with interest over time. This is a way for the government to raise money to pay for things like building roads, schools, or other public projects. Because they are backed by the government, they are often seen as a secure investment.

## What are the different types of federal government-backed debt instruments?

Federal government-backed debt instruments come in several forms, each with its own features and purposes. The most common type is Treasury securities, which include Treasury bills, Treasury notes, and Treasury bonds. Treasury bills, or T-bills, are short-term securities that mature in a year or less. They are sold at a discount and don't pay interest before they mature; instead, the buyer gets the full face value at maturity. Treasury notes, or T-notes, have longer terms, ranging from two to ten years, and they pay interest every six months. Treasury bonds, or T-bonds, are long-term securities that mature in 20 to 30 years and also pay interest every six months.

Another type of federal government-backed debt instrument is savings bonds, which are designed for individual investors. There are two main types of savings bonds: Series EE and Series I. Series EE bonds earn a fixed rate of interest, while Series I bonds earn a rate that combines a fixed rate and an inflation rate, helping to protect the bond's value against inflation. Savings bonds are meant to be held for at least a year, and if cashed in before five years, the last three months of interest are forfeited. These bonds are a way for people to save money safely over time, with the backing of the federal government ensuring their security.

## What is the difference between Treasury bonds, notes, and bills?

Treasury bonds, notes, and bills are all types of loans that the government takes from people and organizations. The main difference between them is how long it takes for the government to pay back the money. Treasury bills, or T-bills, are short-term loans that last for less than a year. They are sold at a lower price than their face value, and when they mature, you get the full face value back. This means you earn money from the difference. Treasury notes, or T-notes, are medium-term loans that last from two to ten years. They pay you interest every six months until they mature, and then you get the full amount back. Treasury bonds, or T-bonds, are long-term loans that last from 20 to 30 years. Like T-notes, they pay interest every six months until they mature.

Another difference is how they pay interest. T-bills don't pay interest until they mature; instead, you earn money from the difference between the price you paid and the face value you get back. Both T-notes and T-bonds pay interest regularly, every six months. This means that if you need a steady income from your investment, T-notes and T-bonds might be better for you. But if you want to get all your money back quickly and don't mind waiting for the interest, T-bills could be a good choice. All these options are safe because they are backed by the government, but they suit different needs depending on how long you want to wait and how you want to get your money back.

By using these debt instruments in this way, the government can help make sure the economy stays balanced and healthy.
