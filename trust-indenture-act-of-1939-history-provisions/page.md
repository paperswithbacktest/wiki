## What is the Trust Indenture Act of 1939?

The Trust Indenture Act of 1939 is a law in the United States that protects people who invest in bonds. It makes sure that bond issuers follow certain rules when they sell bonds to the public. The main goal of this law is to make sure that investors get important information about the bonds they are buying and that their money is safe.

The law requires that a special contract, called a trust indenture, is used when bonds are issued. This contract is between the bond issuer and a trustee, who looks out for the investors' interests. The trustee makes sure the issuer follows the rules and takes action if the issuer does not. This helps to keep the bond market fair and trustworthy for everyone involved.
