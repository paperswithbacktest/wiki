## What is a sub-sovereign obligation?

A sub-sovereign obligation is a type of debt or financial commitment that is issued by a government entity below the national level, such as a state, province, or city. This means that instead of the whole country being responsible for the debt, it's the smaller government within the country that has to pay it back.

These obligations are used by local governments to fund projects like building schools, roads, or hospitals. They can borrow money by issuing bonds, which are like IOUs, promising to pay back the borrowed money with interest over time. This helps them to carry out important projects without needing to wait for funds from the national government.

## How does a sub-sovereign obligation differ from a sovereign obligation?

A sub-sovereign obligation is a type of debt taken on by smaller parts of a country, like states or cities. This means that if a city wants to build a new school, it can borrow money and promise to pay it back over time. The responsibility to pay back this debt falls on the local government, not the whole country. This helps local areas to do important projects without waiting for money from the national government.

On the other hand, a sovereign obligation is a debt taken on by the national government of a country. When a country borrows money, it's the entire nation that's responsible for paying it back. This kind of debt can be used for big national projects, like improving the country's defense or building highways that connect different parts of the country. The key difference is that sovereign obligations involve the whole country, while sub-sovereign obligations are the responsibility of smaller government units within the country.
