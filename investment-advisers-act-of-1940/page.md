## What is the Investment Advisers Act of 1940?

The Investment Advisers Act of 1940 is a law in the United States that regulates people and companies who give advice about investing money. It was created to protect people who use investment advisers by making sure these advisers are honest and follow certain rules. The law says that investment advisers need to register with the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) or state regulators, depending on how much money they manage. This helps keep track of them and makes sure they are following the rules.

The Act also has rules about what investment advisers can and cannot do. For example, they must always put their clients' interests first and not do anything that could hurt their clients, like taking bribes or giving bad advice on purpose. They also have to be clear about any conflicts of interest they might have. This means they need to tell their clients if there's anything that could affect their advice, like if they get paid more for recommending certain investments. By following these rules, the Act helps make sure that people can trust their investment advisers to help them make good choices with their money.
