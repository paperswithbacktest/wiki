## What are put options and how do they work?

Put options are financial contracts that give the buyer the right, but not the obligation, to sell a specific asset, like a stock, at a set price before a certain date. This set price is called the strike price. The buyer pays a fee, called a premium, to the seller for this right. If the stock's price drops below the strike price, the buyer can use the put option to sell the stock at the higher strike price, making a profit from the difference.

For example, imagine you buy a put option for a stock that is currently trading at $50, with a strike price of $45 and an expiration date one month away. You pay a premium of $2 for this option. If the stock price falls to $40 before the expiration date, you can use your put option to sell the stock at $45. You would then make a profit of $3 per share ($5 from the difference between the strike price and the market price, minus the $2 premium you paid). If the stock price stays above $45, you would not use the option and would lose the $2 premium.

## How do you determine the strike price and expiration date for a put option?

When you're [picking](/wiki/asset-class-picking) a strike price for a put option, you need to think about where you think the stock price will go. If you think the stock will drop a lot, you might choose a strike price that's lower than the current price. This way, if the stock falls below that price, you can sell it at the higher strike price and make money. But if you think the stock will only drop a little, you might pick a strike price that's closer to the current price. The closer the strike price is to the current price, the less you'll pay for the option, but you'll also make less money if the stock falls.

Choosing the expiration date is also important. If you think the stock will drop soon, you might pick a shorter expiration date, like a few weeks or a month. This can be cheaper, but you have less time for the stock to move. If you think it might take longer for the stock to drop, you might choose a longer expiration date, like a few months. This gives you more time, but it will cost more. It's all about balancing how much you want to spend with how long you think it will take for the stock to move the way you expect.
