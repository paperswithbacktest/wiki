## What is Free Cash Flow (FCF)?

Free Cash Flow (FCF) is the money a company has left after it pays for its operating expenses and the cost of maintaining or expanding its assets. It's like the extra money in your pocket after you've paid all your bills and bought what you need to keep your home running smoothly. For a business, this money can be used to pay down debt, return money to shareholders through dividends or stock buybacks, or reinvest in new projects.

FCF is important because it shows how well a company is managing its money. If a company has a lot of free cash flow, it means it's generating more cash than it needs to run the business. This is a good sign because it shows the company is financially healthy and has the flexibility to grow or weather tough times. On the other hand, if a company has little or negative free cash flow, it might struggle to keep up with its expenses or invest in future growth.

## What is EBITDA?

EBITDA stands for Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization. It's a way to measure a company's profit by looking at how much money it makes before taking away the costs of interest on loans, taxes, and the gradual reduction in value of its assets over time (that's depreciation and amortization). Think of it as figuring out how much money a business makes from its main activities, without worrying about how it's financed or the age of its equipment.

EBITDA is useful because it gives a clearer picture of a company's operating performance. By not including interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization, it shows how well the core business is doing, without the influence of things like how the company is funded or the age of its machinery. This can be helpful when comparing companies in the same industry, as it levels the playing field by removing these variable costs. However, it's not a perfect measure because it doesn't account for the money needed to replace old equipment or pay off debts, which are important for understanding a company's overall financial health.

## How is Free Cash Flow calculated?

Free Cash Flow (FCF) is calculated by starting with the company's operating cash flow, which is the money coming in from the company's main business activities. From this, you subtract the capital expenditures, which are the costs of buying or maintaining things like buildings, equipment, or technology that the company needs to keep running or grow. So, FCF is basically what's left after the company pays for its day-to-day operations and the big purchases needed to keep the business going.

For example, if a company makes $100,000 from its operations and spends $30,000 on new equipment, the free cash flow would be $70,000. This money can be used for things like paying off debts, giving money back to shareholders, or saving for future projects. FCF is important because it shows how much cash a company has to do these things, which can tell you a lot about its financial health and flexibility.

## How is EBITDA calculated?

EBITDA is calculated by starting with a company's net income, which is the profit after all expenses are paid. Then, you add back the money the company paid for interest on loans, the taxes it paid, and the costs of depreciation and amortization. Depreciation is the gradual decrease in value of things like buildings and machines, and amortization is similar but usually for things like patents or software. So, EBITDA shows the profit from the company's main business activities before these costs are taken out.

For example, if a company's net income is $50,000, and it paid $5,000 in interest, $10,000 in taxes, $3,000 in depreciation, and $2,000 in amortization, you would add these back to the net income. So, the EBITDA would be $50,000 + $5,000 + $10,000 + $3,000 + $2,000, which equals $70,000. This number helps show how well the company is doing at its core business, without the influence of how it's financed or the age of its assets.

## How do investors use FCF and EBITDA in valuation models?

Investors use Free Cash Flow (FCF) in valuation models to figure out how much a company is worth based on the cash it can generate. They look at FCF because it shows the real money a company has after paying for everything it needs to run and grow. By using FCF, investors can see if a company has enough money to pay off debts, give back to shareholders, or invest in new projects. One common way to use FCF in valuation is the Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) model, where investors guess how much cash a company will make in the future and then figure out what that cash is worth today. If a company has a lot of FCF, it might be seen as more valuable because it has the flexibility to grow or handle tough times.

EBITDA is also used by investors to value a company, but it focuses more on the company's profit from its main business activities before taking away costs like interest, taxes, and the wear and tear of its equipment. Investors like EBITDA because it helps them compare companies in the same industry more easily, without worrying about how each company is financed or the age of its assets. They often use EBITDA multiples to value a company, where they compare the company's EBITDA to its market value or the price of its stock. This can give investors a quick way to see if a company is a good deal compared to others in its industry. But, because EBITDA doesn't show everything, like the money needed to replace old equipment or pay off debts, investors usually look at it along with other measures like FCF to get a fuller picture of a company's value.

## What are the limitations of using FCF as a financial metric?

Using Free Cash Flow (FCF) as a financial metric has some limitations. One big one is that FCF can change a lot from year to year. It depends on things like how much a company spends on new equipment or buildings, which can be different each year. This means FCF might not give a clear picture of a company's long-term financial health if you only look at it for one year. Also, FCF can be affected by how a company handles its money, like if it's delaying payments or speeding up collections, which might make FCF look better than it really is.

Another limitation is that FCF doesn't tell you everything about a company's financial situation. For example, it doesn't show if a company has a lot of debt it needs to pay off. Even if a company has high FCF, it might still have trouble if it has big debts coming due soon. FCF also doesn't include things like interest or taxes, which are important costs for a company. So, while FCF is helpful for understanding a company's cash situation, it's not the whole story, and investors need to look at other financial metrics too to get a full picture.

## What are the limitations of using EBITDA as a financial metric?

One big limitation of using EBITDA as a financial metric is that it doesn't show the whole picture of a company's money situation. EBITDA doesn't include costs like interest on loans, taxes, and the wear and tear of equipment. These are important costs that can affect how much money a company really has. For example, a company might have high EBITDA, but if it has a lot of debt, it might struggle to pay off those loans. So, while EBITDA can be helpful to see how well a company's main business is doing, it doesn't tell you if the company can keep running smoothly.

Another limitation is that EBITDA can be easily manipulated. Companies might change how they report things to make their EBITDA look better. For example, they might delay buying new equipment, which would lower their costs and make EBITDA look higher. But this doesn't mean the company is doing better; it just means they're not spending money on things they need. So, while EBITDA is useful for comparing companies in the same industry, it's important to use it along with other financial metrics to really understand a company's health.

## What is Understanding Free Cash Flow (FCF)?

Free Cash Flow (FCF) is a financial metric that represents the cash a company generates after accounting for capital expenditures required to sustain or expand its asset base. Unlike accounting profits, which might include non-cash revenues and expenses, Free Cash Flow provides a clearer picture of actual cash that is both earned and retained by a company, thus making it an 'unencumbered' monetary indicator.

FCF is a crucial measure for evaluating a company's financial health and decision-making flexibility. This metric reveals the amount of cash available to a company for potential reinvestment into the business, distribution of dividends to shareholders, and reduction of debt. High levels of Free Cash Flow can indicate that a company is capable of generating sufficient revenue to fund these initiatives, implying robustness in financial standing.

To compute Free Cash Flow, start with the net income derived from the company's income statement. Adjust this figure for non-cash expenses like depreciation and amortization, which do not directly affect cash flow but do reduce net income. Additionally, modifications for changes in working capital—such as accounts receivable, accounts payable, and inventory levels—are necessary, as these reflect the cash tied up in or released from ongoing operations. Finally, subtract capital expenditures, which are funds used by the company to purchase or upgrade physical assets like property, industrial buildings, or equipment, as these represent cash outflows not reflected in net income directly.

The formula for Free Cash Flow is often expressed as:

$$
\text{FCF} = \text{Net Income} + \text{Depreciation and Amortization} - \text{Change in Working Capital} - \text{Capital Expenditures}
$$

This straightforward representation of cash flow aligns closely with operating cash flows and provides valuable insights beyond what might be indicated by earnings metrics alone. Understanding and utilizing Free Cash Flow provides stakeholders with a reliable measure of a company's natural financial strength and operational efficiency.

## What is EBITDA and how can it be understood?

EBITDA, an acronym for Earnings Before Interest, Tax, Depreciation, and Amortization, is a financial metric that offers a clear view of a company's operational profitability before the influence of non-operational factors. By isolating earnings from interest, tax, depreciation, and amortization, EBITDA provides a measure of a company’s core profitability from its primary business activities. This focus on operational earnings allows investors and analysts to evaluate a company’s performance regardless of its capital structure, tax regime, or investment approaches.

Mathematically, EBITDA is calculated as follows:

$$

\text{EBITDA} = \text{Net Income} + \text{Interest} + \text{Taxes} + \text{Depreciation} + \text{Amortization} 
$$

The exclusion of interest and taxes ensures that the metric reflects purely operational performance, independent of financial and tax strategies. Excluding depreciation and amortization expenses, which are non-cash charges related to past capital investments, further refines this focus, emphasizing the ongoing operational earnings power.

EBITDA is particularly valuable for comparative analysis. By standardizing earnings metrics across different companies, it becomes easier to compare businesses across various industries or those employing different accounting practices. This comparability is crucial in industries such as telecommunications, energy, and manufacturing—sectors characterized by substantial capital expenditures and associated non-cash charges.

In capital-intensive industries, EBITDA is a favored metric because it aligns closely with cash flow without being an actual cash measure. This is because these industries often have significant depreciation and amortization costs, related to their large-scale physical assets, which can distort standard earnings measures like Net Income. Consequently, EBITDA provides an uncomplicated view of profitability, useful in evaluating companies with heavy asset bases and varying capital allocations.

## What are the key differences between FCF and EBITDA?

Free Cash Flow (FCF) and Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization (EBITDA) serve different roles in financial analysis, providing distinct insights into a company's financial health and operational efficiency.

Free Cash Flow offers a more accurate representation of available cash by accounting for capital expenditures. FCF is calculated as:

$$

\text{FCF} = \text{Operating Cash Flow} - \text{Capital Expenditures}
$$

This calculation includes deductions for capital expenditures, ensuring that the available cash reflects actual [liquidity](/wiki/liquidity-risk-premium) that a company can use for dividends, debt repayment, or reinvestment. This comprehensive insight aids investors in evaluating a company's capacity to generate cash from its operations after necessary investments to maintain or expand its asset base.

In contrast, EBITDA does not take capital expenditures into account, which can lead to a less accurate portrayal of a company's financial health. By excluding depreciation and amortization, EBITDA isolates operational profitability from financing and accounting decisions. This exclusion can sometimes result in misleading conclusions, as significant cash outflows related to capital investments are ignored. The formula for EBITDA is:

$$

\text{EBITDA} = \text{Net Income} + \text{Interest} + \text{Taxes} + \text{Depreciation} + \text{Amortization}
$$

Due to its exclusion of capital expenditures, EBITDA is often preferred in merger and acquisition scenarios. It provides a standardized measure for comparing the profitability of companies regardless of their capital structure or investment strategies. This feature makes it a valuable tool for assessing operational efficiency and potential profitability when considering mergers and acquisitions.

On the other hand, FCF is generally considered a more transparent metric. It emphasizes a company's ability to generate cash, providing a fuller picture of financial health that includes the impacts of capital expenditures. This transparency can be crucial for investors seeking to understand how much cash a company truly has at its disposal for growth and shareholder returns.

Overall, both FCF and EBITDA serve significant roles in financial analysis, but users must be aware of their inherent differences and limitations. These insights are vital for making informed decisions about investments, particularly when evaluating companies with substantial capital investment needs or varied capital structures.
